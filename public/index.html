What is pixel art?

Shortest definition ever:
A grid of colored pixels where each pixel is important

How did pixel art come about?

Back in the day, computers weren't as good and pixel art
was our best option for displaying graphics

Now we have other options

But pixel art survives as an art form

How do I get started?

The best way to learn is to make some yourself, so...
remember Space Invaders?

Activity 1: Create a Space Invader

  • White on black
  • 8px tall, 8-16px wide
  • Should have "eyes" and probably "fangs"
  • All one shape (no stray pixels)

Frames

A frame is a still image

By displaying frames in quick succession we create the illusion of movement

Activity 2: Animate your Space Invader

  • Duplicate the first frame of your invader
  • Modify the second frame to make it wiggle
  • Set framerate to 4 FPS

Making a sprite

Let's try making a more complex sprite

Creating an outline

The first step is to get the basic lines and colors down

Creating an outline

There are two basic strategies for doing this:

1. Fiddle with some lines, then fill them in

2. Sculpt some color blobs, then outline them

Creating an outline

In reality, you should mix these two techniques

(This is what I actually did)

Linework

Careful! Small changes can imply very different shapes

Activity 3: Create a sprite

  • Create an outline of any 3D object
  • Fill it with the fewest possible colors
  • Try to stay within 24x24 (32x32 max)
  • Keep your lines in mind!

Coloring a sprite

Once we have the basic shape and colors, we want to add highlights and shadows to make our sprite appear 3D

Coloring a sprite

The easiest way to do this is by varying lightness (a slightly whiter color for highlights, slightly blacker for shadows)

(Note: highlights/shadows depend on the light source. In general light should be above and at an angle to your sprite)

Coloring a sprite

The decision of how many colors to use is up to you!

But in general, only add a color if it will
meaningfully contribute to your piece

Color ramps

The colors you choose for highlights/shadows can be arranged from dark to light to form a color ramp

This color ramp was made just by varying lightness, but we can make our color ramps more exciting...

Color ramps

In real life light and shadows change the hue of an object

This is called hue shifting

Color ramps

Highlights tend to be more yellow,
shadows tend to be more blue

Color ramps

Here's my froggie with and without hue shifting

The frog on the left is more visually consistent

By hue shifting we also get to reuse a color—the lightest green is the same color as the lightest yellow

Color ramps

A complete palette is just a bunch of color ramps

By finding colors that can be reused in multiple color ramps, you lower your color count and increase consistency

Activity 4: Color your sprite

  • Add shadows and highlights
  • Organize your colors into color ramps
  • Be on the lookout for reusable colors
  • Add any final details
