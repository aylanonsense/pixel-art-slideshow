Making a sprite

Let's try making a more complex sprite

Creating an outline

The first step is to get the basic lines and colors down

Creating an outline

There are two basic strategies for doing this:

1. Fiddle with some lines, then fill them in

2. Sculpt some color blobs, then outline them

Creating an outline

In reality, you should mix these two techniques

(This is what I actually did)

Linework

Careful! Small changes can imply very different shapes

Activity 3: Create a sprite

  • Create an outline of any 3D object
  • Fill it with the fewest possible colors
  • Try to stay within 24x24 (32x32 max)
  • Keep your lines in mind!

Coloring a sprite

Once we have the basic shape and colors, we want to add highlights and shadows to make our sprite appear 3D

Coloring a sprite

Highlights/shadows depend on the light source. In general light should be above and at an angle to your sprite

(Like the sun!)

Coloring a sprite

The decision of how many colors to use is up to you!

But in general, only add a color if it will
meaningfully contribute to your piece

Color ramps

The colors you choose for highlights/shadows can be arranged from dark to light to form a color ramp

You should think about your colors in the context
of the color ramps they are a part of

Color ramps

In real life light and shadows change the hue of an object

This is called hue shifting and we want it in our color ramps!

Color ramps

Highlights tend to be more yellow,
shadows tend to be more blue

Color ramps

Here's my froggie with and without hue shifting

The frog on the left is more visually consistent

by hue shifting I also reuse a color—the lightest green is the same color as the lightest yellow

Color ramps

A complete palette is just a bunch of color ramps

By finding colors that can be reused in multiple color ramps, you lower your color count and increase consistency

  • Organize your colors into color ramps
  • Be on the lookout for reusable colors
